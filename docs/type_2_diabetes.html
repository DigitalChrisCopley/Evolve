Type 2 Diabetes

Type 2 diabetes is a common condition that causes the level of sugar (glucose) in the blood to become too high.

It can cause symptoms like excessive thirst, needing to pee a lot and tiredness. It can also increase your risk of getting serious problems with your eyes, heart and nerves.

It's a lifelong condition that can affect your everyday life. You may need to change your diet, take medicines and have regular check-ups.

It's caused by problems with a chemical in the body (hormone) called insulin. It's often linked to being overweight, inactive or having a family history of type 2 diabetes.

Prevention

The good news is that simple lifestyle measures can significantly reduce your risk. Healthy daily habits can be effective in preventing or delaying the onset of type 2 diabetes. To help prevent type 2 diabetes and its complications, people should:

  • achieve and maintain a healthy body weight
  • be physically active - doing at least 30 minutes of regular, moderate-intensity activity on most days. More activity is required for weight control
  • eat a health diet, avoiding saturated fats, and
  • avoid tobacco use - smoking increases the risk of diabetes and cardiovascular disease
Signs and Symptoms

As well as a healthy lifestyle, it's important that we're aware and detect early warning signs of type 2 Diabetes as the symptoms do not necessarily make us feel unwell.

Symptoms of type 2 diabetes include:

  • peeing more than usual, particularly at night
  • feeling thirsty all the time
  • feeling very tired
  • losing weight without trying to
  • itching around your penis or vagina, or repeatedly getting thrush
  • cuts or wounds taking longer to heal
  • blurred vision
IMAGE

It's also important to keep up with your regular eye checks as your optician will also be able to pick up on any early warning signs.

You're more at risk of developing type 2 diabetes if you:

  • are over 40 (or 25 for south Asian people)
  • have a close relative with diabetes (such as a parent, brother or sister)
  • are overweight or obese
  • are of Asian, African-Caribbean or black African origin (even if you were born in the UK)

What to do if you have concerns that you may have Type 2 Diabetes

The earlier diabetes is diagnosed and treatment started, the better. Early treatment reduces your risk of other health problems. Here is a link to further information on the other health problems that are linked with Diabetes:

https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/type-2-diabetes/health-problems/

Therefore, it's important to contact your GP if:

  • you have any of the symptoms of type 3 diabetes outlined in this information sheet
  • you're worried you may have a higher risk of getting type 2 diabetes

The GP will check your urine and arrange a blood test to check your blood sugar levels. It usually takes about 1 to 2 days for the results to come back.

This link explains more about what happens if you go to your GP with concerns:

https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/type-2-diabetes/getting-diagnosed/

Although we're living in difficult time currently, if you have Diabetes it is important that you don't neglect your routine contact with your GP/Diabetes Specialist Nurse and also to ensure you have your free seasonal flu vaccination .
