Influenza - Flu
Influenza, commonly known as the "flu," is an extremely contagious respiratory illness caused by influenza A or B viruses. It is usually most frequently in winter and early spring. The flu virus attacks the body by spreading through the upper and/or lower respiratory tract.

https://www.nhs.uk/live-well/healthy-body/facts-about-flu-and-the-flu-vaccine/

What's the difference between a cold and flu?

 

 

The common cold and flu are both contagious viral infections of the respiratory tract. Although the symptoms can be similar, flu is much worse. A cold may make you down a bit, but the flu can make you struggle to get out of bed.

Congestion, sore throat, and sneezing are common with colds. Both cold and flu may bring coughing, headache, and chest discomfort. With the flu, though, you are likely to have a high temperature for several days and have body aches, fatigue, and weakness. Symptoms of the flu also tend to come on abruptly. Usually, complications from colds are relatively minor, but a case of flu can lead to a life-threatening illness such as pneumonia.

https://www.nhs.uk/conditions/flu/

More than 100 types of cold viruses are known, and new strains of flu evolve every few years. Since both diseases are viral, antibiotics cannot conquer cold or flu. Remember: Antibiotics only treat bacterial infections.

Two antiviral medications are available to treat flu. But there are no medications that specifically defeat the common cold. Antibiotics may be helpful only if there is a secondary bacterial infection.

The influenza Vaccination is the most effective measure at preventing influenza and its severe outcomes. Recent studies show the vaccine can reduce the risk of influenza by about 60% among the overall population. Please see the NHS link below with regards to the vaccine.
